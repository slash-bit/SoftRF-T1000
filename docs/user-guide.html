SenseCap T1000E Card User Guide

Power On

When you first power on the device, the LED will be solid green, indicating that the battery power is good and the device is NOT airborne. The device will beep according to the battery charge level (see below). After battery beeps you will hear Operating Mode indication beeps.

Battery Charge Status

After switching the device on, the battery charge status is indicated by a sequence of short beeps:

BeepsBattery Level
5x Beep95% or more
4x Beep75% or more
3x Beep55% or more
2x Beep35% or more
1x Beep15% or more
Low Battery Warning If the battery is less than 15% charged, you will hear a constant beep for one second after switching the device on. A LOW battery charge level is also indicated by a rapidly flashing LED.

Radio Mode

This device supports three protocols: LEGACY (FLARM), FANET and ADS-L. The selected protocol is indicated by the following beep sequences after the battery charge beeps:

SoundProtocol
Two long tonesFLARM mode
Two short tonesFANET mode
One longer toneADS-L mode

When Airborne

Slow Blink — The LED blinks slowly to indicate the device is in airborne mode.

Buzzer Sound Volume

Single-click the button to toggle between the buzzer volume levels: FULL → OFF

VolumeSound Indicator
FULLTwo rising tones
OFFTwo lowering tones

Changing Settings on the Go

Most of device settings are configurable from the WebApp Configurator more information here Configurator

Changing Settings via Settings File

All settings are stored in a settings.json file on the device's USB storage. To edit it:

  1. Connect the powered-on T1000E card to your PC/Mac via USB.
  2. If connecting for the first time, you will be prompted to format the drive. Format it.
  3. After formatting, reconnect the device. A storage drive window will open.
  4. Double-click the button to initialize the settings file. This writes a default settings.json and reboots the device.
  5. Reconnect the device to your PC/Mac. Open the settings.json file in any text editor (e.g., Notepad).
  6. Edit the values you want to change, save the file, then power-cycle the device to apply.

Example settings.json:

{
  "class": "SOFTRF",
  "protocol": "FANET",
  "altprotocol": "NONE",
  "band": "EU",
  "aircraft_type": "PARAGLIDER",
  "aircraft_id": "88BB50",
  "id_method": "DEVICE",
  "alarm": "LATEST",
  "alarm_filter": "NONE",
  "txpower": "FULL",
  "volume": "FULL",
  "ignore_id": "000000",
  "alarmlog": "YES",
  "auto_sos": "NO",
  "logflight": "AIRBORNE",
  "loginterval": 4,
  "igc_pilot": "Chuck Yeager",
  "igc_type": "Ozone",
  "igc_reg": "Delta4",
  "fanet_name": "Chuck",
  "debug_flags": "000000",
  "sw_version": "SoftRF-MB177-VB008j-dev"
}
Applying Changes After saving the file, power off the device by long-pressing the button, then power it back on to apply the new settings. The sw_version field is written by the firmware — do not change it manually.

Collision Alarms

SoftRF analyses the positions and trajectories of nearby aircraft and issues alarms at three urgency levels:

LevelTime to collisionBuzzer
Level 1 (Low)≤ 19 seconds1 beep
Level 2 (Medium)≤ 13 seconds2 faster beeps
Level 3 (Urgent)≤ 9 seconds5 quick beeps

If multiple aircraft are a threat simultaneously, beeps are doubled (levels 1–2) or extended to 7 (level 3).

Ground Testing SoftRF does not generate collision warnings while on the ground. It does give warnings for one minute after boot and first GNSS fix, to allow testing before a flight.

Flight Logging

The T1000E Card logs IGC-format flight tracks to its internal flash storage. Logs are accessible by connecting the device to a PC/Mac via USB.

  • Set "logflight" to "AIRBORNE" or "ALWAYS" in settings.json to enable logging.
  • Set "loginterval" to control the fix recording interval (e.g., 4 seconds).
  • Fill in "igc_pilot", "igc_type", and "igc_reg" so that logs are identified correctly on flight sharing sites.

Flight logs are secured with G-records and accepted on Weglide and OLC, but are not IGC-certified.

Alarm events can be logged separately by setting "alarmlog": "YES". The resulting alarmlog.txt can be visualised using the Alarm Log Viewer tool.

Updating Firmware

  1. Download the latest .uf2 firmware file from the binaries page.
  2. Connect the T1000E Card to your PC/Mac via USB while powered on.
  3. Delete any large files on the drive to free space, then eject the drive (keep the cable connected).
  4. Double-click the RESET button — a drive named NRF52BOOT appears.
  5. Drag the .uf2 file onto the NRF52BOOT drive.
  6. Wait approximately 30 seconds for the transfer and automatic reboot.
  7. The device will restart with the new firmware. Reconnect and verify settings are intact.

FANET Features

When operating in FANET mode, the T1000E broadcasts position and status data over the FANET radio network — a low-power mesh used by paragliders, hang-gliders, and mountain sport pilots. Your position, name, and status are visible in real time on compatible apps and devices including Skytraxx, Oudie N, XC Guide, GXAircom, BurnAir Map, and Puretrack.

Ground Tracking (2D) vs Flight Tracking (3D)

The device automatically switches between two tracking modes depending on whether it detects movement consistent with flight:

ModeWhen activeWhat is broadcast
Flight Tracking (3D) Device is airborne — altitude and speed above threshold Full 3D position: latitude, longitude, and altitude. Shown as a moving aircraft symbol on compatible maps.
Ground Tracking (2D) Device is on the ground or stationary 2D position: latitude and longitude only, with a ground status flag. Shown as a ground symbol with a status label on compatible maps.

The transition between modes is automatic. No pilot action is needed.

Ground Tracking States

While in Ground Tracking (2D) mode the device broadcasts one of several status values that describe what the pilot is doing on the ground. These states are visible as labels or icons on Skytraxx, Oudie N, XC Guide, and BurnAir Map:

StateMeaning
FlyingAirborne — shown in flight tracking mode
ActiveOn the ground, moving (walking to launch, packing up)
IdleOn the ground, stationary
FinishedFlight completed, pilot has landed and is done for the day
Need a RidePilot is landed out and needs retrieval
Need Technical SupportPilot has a technical issue (equipment problem, not an emergency)
DistressEmergency — pilot needs urgent help. See SOS section below.

SOS — Pilot in Distress

The SOS feature broadcasts an emergency signal when a pilot needs urgent assistance after a crash or injury.

Manual activation

Double-click the button while on the ground to toggle the SOS state on or off. When SOS is active:

  • The device switches to Ground Tracking (2D) mode and broadcasts the Distress ground state.
  • A FANET message is broadcast over the radio network: "SOS! Pilot in Distress" together with the pilot's GPS coordinates, so nearby pilots and rescue teams can locate you.
  • The Distress status and coordinates appear on BurnAir Map, Skytraxx, Oudie N, and XC Guide.

To cancel SOS, double-click the button again. The device returns to normal operation.

Automatic activation (auto_sos)

If "auto_sos": "YES" is set in settings.json, the device monitors for a crash event (sudden stop after airborne movement). If a potential crash is detected:

  1. The device starts beeping to alert the pilot that SOS will activate.
  2. The pilot has 3 minutes to double-click the button to confirm they are OK and cancel the countdown.
  3. If no action is taken within 3 minutes, SOS activates automatically — broadcasting Distress status and the location message as described above.
Test SOS carefully Always cancel a test SOS activation immediately. Leaving a Distress broadcast active will alert other pilots and rescue services in the area.

FANET Name

The "fanet_name" field in settings.json sets a short pilot name that is broadcast with every FANET packet. This name appears as a label next to your position dot on:

  • Skytraxx device screens
  • Oudie N display
  • XC Guide app map
  • GXAircom app
  • BurnAir Map
  • Puretrack Map

Keep it short (up to ~10 characters) so it fits cleanly on screen. Example: "fanet_name": "Chuck".

FANET Messages

The T1000E can send and receive short text messages over the FANET radio network. Messages travel directly between devices without any internet connection — useful in the mountains where cellular coverage is poor or absent.

Compatible devices and apps for FANET messaging:

  • T1000E (this device) — send/receive via the XC Guide app over BLE
  • T-Echo (Moshe Braner's SoftRF build) — send/receive with e-paper display
  • GXAircom app
  • Skytraxx devices
  • Oudie N
  • XC Guide app

Messages are limited to approximately 60–80 characters. They are broadcast to all nearby FANET devices within radio range (typically 5–15 km in open terrain).

Weather Stations

FANET weather stations (installed at launch sites and mountain passes) broadcast local conditions over the FANET network including wind speed, wind direction, temperature, and humidity. The T1000E receives these broadcasts automatically whenever it is in FANET mode.

Received weather data can be displayed on the map in XC Guide, showing wind arrows and station data at the station's location. No internet connection is required — the data is received directly from the station over the air.

Other apps may gain support for FANET weather display in future updates.
